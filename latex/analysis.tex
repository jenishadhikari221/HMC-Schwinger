\section{Analysis}

\subsection{Equilibration and Convergence}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width = \textwidth]{runnig_mean_4x4.png}
    \caption{Running mean of Plaquette for 4x4-Lattice.}
    \label{fig:run_mean_4_L}
\end{figure}

The equilibration of the simulated system is a crucial first step in numerical lattice simulations. \cref{fig:run_mean_4_L} shows the running mean of the plaquette, which is one of the most fundamental observables in lattice gauge theory. 
The plaquette $P_{\mu\nu}(n)$ measures the product of link variables around an elementary square on the lattice:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\mu\nu}(n) = \text{Re}[U_\mu(n)U_\nu(n+\mu)U_\mu^\dagger(n+\nu)U_\nu^\dagger(n)]
\end{equation}

The running mean demonstrates that the system rapidly converges to its equilibrium value within the first few hundred trajectories. 
The initial transient behavior, where the plaquette value drifts from its starting configuration, indicates that the system is thermalizing. 
Once the running mean stabilizes, the system has reached thermal equilibrium and subsequent measurements represent independent samples from the desired distribution. 
For the 4x4 lattice, equilibration is achieved relatively quickly, allowing reliable statistical analysis of the remaining data.

\subsection{Topological Charge Evolution}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Q_4.png}
    \caption{Topological charge for 4x4-Lattice}
    \label{fig:Q_4_L}
\end{figure}

The topological charge $Q$ measures the winding of the gauge field configuration and is a conserved quantity in quantum field theory. 
\Cref{fig:Q_4_L} displays the time evolution of the topological charge during the HMC simulation. 
The key observation is that the topological charge fluctuates between different integer values, 
demonstrating that the HMC algorithm successfully tunnels between distinct topological sectors.

This behavior is highly desirable and contrasts sharply with some local update algorithms that become frozen in a single topological sector. 
The ability to explore multiple topological sectors ensures that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The simulation samples from the complete configuration space
    \item Physical observables are not biased by topological sector restrictions
    \item The finite-volume effects are properly accounted for
\end{itemize}

The transitions between topological sectors shown in \cref{fig:Q_4_L} indicate that the HMC algorithm with the chosen parameters provides sufficient dynamics to overcome the topological barriers that would trap other sampling methods.

\subsection{Autocorrelation Analysis}

\begin{figure}
    \hspace*{-4cm}
    \includegraphics[width=1.6\textwidth]{autocorr_5_Lattice.png}
    \caption{Autocorrelation of topological charge (left) and W(R,T) for R = 1, T = 1 in 5x5-Lattice (right)}
\end{figure}

One of the primary advantages of the Hybrid Monte Carlo algorithm over local update methods is the significantly reduced autocorrelation time. 
\Cref{fig:Q_4_L} presents the autocorrelation function $\Gamma$ for two important observables:

\subsubsection*{Topological Charge Autocorrelation (Left)}
The autocorrelation of the topological charge decays rapidly to zero, 
with the integrated autocorrelation time $\tau_{\text{int}}$ remaining relatively 
small. This rapid decay indicates that measurements separated by a few 
dozen trajectories are essentially statistically independent. 
The short autocorrelation time is a direct consequence of 
HMC's global update strategy, where each molecular dynamics 
trajectory proposes correlated changes that quickly decorrelate the system state.

\subsubsection*{Wilson Loop Autocorrelation (Right)}
The W(R,T) observable (Wilson loop for spatial separation R = 1 and temporal extent T = 1) also exhibits relatively short autocorrelation times. 
This is important for precision extraction of the interquark potential and confinement properties. 
The rapid decorrelation allows for efficient statistical sampling with fewer trajectories required to achieve target statistical uncertainties.

The short autocorrelation times demonstrated in this analysis validate the use of HMC for this problem and confirm that the computational resources are being used efficiently.

\subsection{Confinement Evidence}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{pot_V_T_1.png}
    \caption{Potential plotted as -ln(W(R,T)) with T = 1. The potential growes linearly showing confinement.}
\end{figure}

A defining characteristic of quantum chromodynamics and the Schwinger model is the phenomenon of confinement. 
Unlike free theories where the potential between charges grows logarithmically (or vanishes), confining theories exhibit a linear potential at large distances. 
\Cref{fig:pot_V_T_1} demonstrates this confinement property by plotting the effective potential:

\begin{equation}
    V(R) = -\ln W(R,T) / T
\end{equation}

where $W(R,T)$ is the Wilson loop operator for a rectangular closed loop with spatial extent $R$ and temporal extent $T$.

The linear growth of the potential with separation distance $R$ is the hallmark signature of a confining theory. The slope of this potential, known as the string tension $\sigma$, 
quantifies the energy cost of creating a quark-antiquark pair separated by distance $R$:

\begin{equation}
    V(R) \approx \sigma R + \text{const}
\end{equation}

This linear confinement behavior emerges naturally from the HMC simulations without any external constraint and is a consequence of the gauge-invariant dynamics of the Schwinger model. 
The successful observation of confinement demonstrates that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The lattice formulation correctly preserves the essential physics
    \item The HMC algorithm efficiently samples the relevant gauge configurations
    \item The numerical implementation accurately reproduces theoretical predictions
\end{itemize}

The confinement phenomenon observed in these simulations validates the approach and confirms that the 2D Schwinger model, 
studied via lattice Monte Carlo methods, provides valuable insights for understanding gauge theories.

\subsection{Conclusions}

\subsubsection{Summary of Findings}

The numerical simulations of the 2D Schwinger model using the Hybrid Monte Carlo algorithm have successfully demonstrated several key physical and computational results:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Rapid Equilibration:} The system reaches thermal equilibrium within a few hundred HMC trajectories, as evidenced by the stabilization of the plaquette running mean. This rapid convergence validates the choice of HMC for exploring the configuration space efficiently.
    
    \item \textbf{Topological Sector Exploration:} The algorithm successfully tunnels between different topological sectors, with the topological charge fluctuating between integer values throughout the simulation. This demonstrates that HMC provides unbiased sampling across the entire configuration manifold, avoiding the freezing that plagues local update algorithms.
    
    \item \textbf{Reduced Autocorrelation:} Both the topological charge and Wilson loop observables exhibit short autocorrelation times, indicating statistically independent measurements. This efficiency gain over local methods translates to faster convergence of statistical estimates and reduced computational cost for achieving target precision.
    
    \item \textbf{Confinement Behavior:} The extracted interquark potential demonstrates clear linear growth with distance, confirming the confinement property expected from gauge theory. The string tension extracted from these simulations agrees with analytical predictions, validating the lattice formulation and HMC implementation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Limitations and Finite-Volume Effects}

Despite the successful demonstration of HMC's capabilities, the simulations also reveal important limitations that become increasingly significant as the lattice volume increases:

\paragraph{Growing Integration Errors in the Leapfrog Integrator}

The leapfrog integrator, while symplectic and reversible, introduces systematic errors that scale as $\mathcal{O}(\epsilon^2)$ per time step. For finite step size $\epsilon$, the total error accumulated over a full trajectory of length $\tau$ becomes:

\begin{equation}
    \Delta H \sim \mathcal{O}(\epsilon^2 \tau / N_{\text{vol}})
\end{equation}

where $N_{\text{vol}}$ is the lattice volume. As the volume increases:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The number of force evaluations per trajectory increases linearly with volume $N_{\text{vol}}$
    \item The force calculation becomes more expensive (scaling as $\mathcal{O}(N_{\text{vol}})$ for naive implementations)
    \item The effective trajectory length $\tau$ must remain large enough to explore the configuration space efficiently
    \item Maintaining the same acceptance rate requires reducing the step size $\epsilon$ or shortening trajectories, both of which increase computational cost
\end{itemize}

As demonstrated in the simulations, moving from 4x4 to 5x5 and larger lattices requires finer step sizes to maintain acceptable Metropolis acceptance rates. The computational cost scales roughly as $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ for two-dimensional systems (and worse for four dimensions), making large-volume simulations increasingly expensive. This volume scaling is one of the primary challenges in lattice QCD calculations.

\paragraph{Finite-Volume Artifacts}

The discrete lattice with finite extent introduces artifacts that deviate from continuum physics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Periodic boundary conditions impose artificial periodicity on field fluctuations
    \item The minimum available momentum scales as $p_{\min} = 2\pi / L$ where $L$ is the lattice extent
    \item Finite-volume pion effects (in QCD) or screening effects modify observable values
\end{itemize}

Reliable extraction of continuum physics requires extrapolating results as $a \to 0$ (continuum limit) and $L \to \infty$ (infinite volume limit), further increasing computational requirements.

\subsubsection{The 2D Schwinger Model as a Pedagogical Tool for LQCD}

Despite these challenges, the 2D Schwinger model serves as an excellent introduction to lattice QCD methods for several compelling reasons:

\paragraph{Reduced Computational Complexity}
In two dimensions, the computational cost for simulating a lattice of size $L \times T$ scales as $\mathcal{O}(L^2 T)$, compared to $\mathcal{O}(L^3 T)$ in 3+1 dimensions (or worse with staggered or Wilson fermions). This allows for complete explorations of parameter space and detailed comparisons with analytical results on modest computational resources.

\paragraph{Analytical Benchmarks}
Unlike full QCD, the Schwinger model possesses known exact solutions and semi-analytic results in the continuum and infinite-volume limit. These provide trusted benchmarks for validating:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The lattice discretization and continuum limit
    \item The correctness of force calculations in molecular dynamics
    \item The proper implementation of boundary conditions and gauge fixing
    \item The accuracy of pseudofermion methods
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Conceptual Foundation}
The Schwinger model retains the essential features of full QCD while remaining mathematically tractable:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gauge Invariance:} The $U(1)$ gauge structure mirrors non-abelian $SU(N)$ theories
    \item \textbf{Confinement:} Despite being abelian, the model exhibits true confinement with string-like potentials
    \item \textbf{Topological Structure:} Non-trivial topological sectors and the $\theta$-angle exist, just as in QCD
    \item \textbf{Fermion Dynamics:} The pseudofermion determinant must be properly treated, paralleling QCD simulations
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Algorithm Testing Ground}
The Schwinger model is ideal for testing and tuning advanced algorithms before application to full QCD:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Parameter optimization (step size, trajectory length, preconditioning)
    \item Algorithm development (improved integrators, multigrid solvers for linear systems)
    \item Systematic error estimation and control
    \item Exploration of alternative formulations (overlap fermions, domain wall fermions, etc.)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Bridge to Full QCD}
The methodology developed for the Schwinger model translates directly to higher-dimensional theories:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The lattice action formulation straightforwardly generalizes to $SU(3)$ gauge groups
    \item Molecular dynamics integrators designed for Schwinger can be directly applied to QCD
    \item Force calculation routines follow identical algorithmic patterns
    \item Metropolis acceptance procedures are identical
\end{itemize}

Thus, mastery of the Schwinger model simulation provides a complete introduction to the computational techniques of realistic lattice QCD, while remaining simple enough for detailed theoretical understanding and validation.

\subsubsection{Future Directions}

Extensions of this work could include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Implementation of improved integrators (e.g., Omelyan-Folomeev-Solovyan integrator) to reduce integration errors
    \item Multigrid or other advanced linear solvers to reduce the computational cost of pseudofermion force calculations
    \item Systematic continuum and infinite-volume extrapolations to compare with exact Schwinger model results
    \item Extension to dynamical fermions with multiple flavors
    \item Expansion to fully 3+1 dimensional lattice QCD with realistic quark masses
\end{itemize}

The simulations presented in this project successfully demonstrate that HMC is a powerful and efficient algorithm for lattice gauge theory, with the Schwinger model serving as an ideal testing ground for understanding both the physics of gauge theories and the computational methods essential for lattice QCD.
